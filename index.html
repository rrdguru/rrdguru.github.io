Research

What I Study

Quality is not one thing. That is the starting point of my research. When a consumer evaluates a product, they are actually weighing multiple distinct dimensions simultaneously — functional performance, sensory experience, reliability, symbolic meaning. My work builds frameworks for understanding how these dimensions combine and how they shape brand relationships over time. The central contribution of my doctoral work was developing and validating a scale that captures this multidimensionality: the Customers' Experienced Product Quality (CEPQ) scale. More recently I have been asking whether the same logic applies to higher education. If a university is a service that students evaluate, what does quality mean in that context, and how does it affect their long-term relationship with the institution? The methods are quantitative throughout: structural equation modelling, scale development, longitudinal survey designs across multiple markets.

Published Work

Two peer-reviewed publications form the empirical foundation of the research programme.

Journal Article

Customers' Experienced Product Quality: Scale Development and Validation Across Four Product Categories

Ramesh Roshan Das Guru and M. Paulssen. Develops and validates the multidimensional CEPQ scale across four diverse product categories, establishing sound psychometric properties for each underlying quality dimension. This is the core empirical contribution of the doctoral research programme — it provides the measurement infrastructure for studying how distinct quality dimensions independently influence consumer behaviour.

Conference Proceedings

The Effect of Coercive Power on Supply Chain Inventory Replenishment Decisions

Ramesh Roshan Das Guru, A. Kaboli and R. Glardon. APMS 2014. Examines how power awareness shapes coercive power dynamics and order variability in supply chain settings. Power awareness plays a significant role in the way coercive power is exercised, producing measurable increases in order quantity and order time variability under conditions of imbalanced power with awareness.

Research Pipeline

The published work established the measurement tools. The pipeline work uses those tools to pursue the longitudinal and applied questions that have accumulated since. Three studies are at different stages of completion. None are placeholder — each has data behind it.
Under Preparation

Multidimensional Quality and Long-Run Brand Loyalty: Longitudinal Evidence

Extends the CEPQ framework to trace how individual quality dimensions drive loyalty trajectories over time. This is the natural next step from the scale validation — now that we can measure the dimensions reliably, the question is which ones do the most work in sustaining loyalty, and whether their relative influence shifts across the relationship lifecycle. Data collection is complete; manuscript under preparation.

Under Review

Higher Education as a Service: Quality Perception and Institutional Loyalty in Emerging Markets

Applies the multidimensional quality lens to Indian higher education. The motivating question is whether students form something like brand relationships with their universities — and if so, what drives those relationships. The study draws on the CEPQ logic but adapts it to an education-as-service context where experiential, functional and symbolic dimensions each play distinct roles. Fieldwork conducted across institutions in India.

Data Collection Phase

Sustainable Consumption and Perceived Quality: How Environmental Dimensions Fit the Framework

Investigates whether sustainability attributes function as a distinct quality dimension within the CEPQ framework and how they interact with more established quality perceptions in brand choice. As sustainability becomes a live factor in Indian consumer markets, understanding whether it behaves like other quality dimensions — or differently — has both theoretical and practical implications.

The Growth Arc: Four Phases

1
Phase 1 · 2018 to 2022
Foundation: Geneva Teaching Assistant and HSE Digital Professor
  • Teaching Assistant at University of Geneva GSEM across Branding, Data Driven Decision Making and Research Methods.
  • Availability rated at or near 4.00/4.00 in every single evaluation — a consistent strength from the very beginning.
  • Visiting digital professor at HSE Moscow, teaching R and Python to Russian undergraduates during COVID disruption.
Key data point: DDDM scores improved from 3.43 to 3.61 between 2018 and 2020 — the earliest evidence in the dataset of a clear feedback-to-growth loop.
2
Phase 2 · 2022
First Years at IIM Bodh Gaya: Early Peaks and First Friction
  • POMM IPM Jan 2022: course score 4.74, preparation 4.87. Immediate credibility with undergraduate cohorts.
  • Rural Marketing Mar 2022: instructor score 4.76. The life-skill framing lands hardest in this course.
  • First PGP friction: a proxy attendance incident with a large cohort creates a mid-semester climate shift that the data picks up clearly.
Finding: Undergraduate students respond more strongly to inspiration-led pedagogy. PGP MBA students bring stronger prior expectations and need a different classroom contract. This distinction shapes everything that follows.
3
Phase 3 · 2023
Consolidation and Crisis: The Most Diagnostic Year
  • Record highs in repeat courses: POMM IPM reaches 4.97 preparation, Rural Marketing stays strong.
  • BRM IPM Mar 2023: lowest scores in the dataset — instructor 2.94, respect score 2.50 — following a single classroom incident.
  • Recovery visible by Oct 2023: Brand Management at 4.13 and MBA Foundation Marketing at 4.68 confirm the pedagogical pivot is working.
Critical pattern: Preparation never drops below 3.95 even at the dataset's lowest point. The crisis was entirely a classroom relationship issue, not a knowledge deficit and not a lack of effort. That distinction matters when reading this data.
4
Phase 4 · 2025
Resolution and Mastery: IIM Ranchi Peak
  • Preparation 5.00/5.00, unanimous across all 29 respondents. Zero variance. A first in the entire dataset.
  • Instructor vs peers 4.86/5.00. Exam feedback: 25 out of 29 said they enjoyed it. Zero said useless.
  • Students from engineering and finance backgrounds report permanent shifts in how they understand marketing.
The formula, confirmed: Maximum intellectual rigour plus explicitly protected psychological safety plus consistent interactive tools equals the highest scores in seven years of data. This is now the floor, not the ceiling.
