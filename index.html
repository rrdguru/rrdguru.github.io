Research

What I Study

The core question is deceptively simple: what does quality actually mean to a consumer? Not as a single score, but as a set of distinct, simultaneously experienced dimensions.
My doctoral work built and validated a measurement instrument for this, the CEPQ scale, published in the European Journal of Marketing. Since then the questions have deepened and expanded. How do quality perceptions, satisfaction and brand attachment drive loyalty differently over time? Do these dynamics hold when the product being evaluated is a management education or a tourism destination?

Teaching

Eight Years of Evidence

How I think about teaching
  • I ask students to understand the problem before they reach for a solution. Most courses jump to frameworks. I make people sit with the problem first, interrogate it, articulate what is actually being asked. The framework comes after. That order matters more than most people realise.
  • Critical thinking is not something I mention in a syllabus and move on. It means slowing down in class, questioning the first answer that comes up, refusing to let comfortable explanations sit unchallenged. Students notice fairly quickly that this is a different kind of room.
  • I teach marketing and research as skills, not subjects. A student who has genuinely understood how quality perceptions form will never see a product advertisement the same way again. That permanent shift in how someone reads the world is what a course should leave behind.
  • My industry years are not background colour. Having managed electrical infrastructure in a steel plant, written software, and done quantitative consulting, I bring those contexts into class not as anecdotes but as the actual analytical method.
  • The classroom has to be safe enough for genuine thinking to happen. Rigour and safety are not competing values. The best learning I have witnessed, and the evaluation data bears this out, happens when both are true at the same time.

Teaching in a Greenfield Context

SAU is a newly established SAARC university in its first operational year. The Faculty of Management is a greenfield institution: no inherited curriculum, no established student culture, no prior cohort to benchmark against. The first MBA batch was admitted under conditions that the faculty itself did not control. I was assigned courses outside my primary domain — Indian Ethos and Business Ethics, and an IT Systems Lab — because the institution needed coverage, not because these were my areas of expertise.

The first-semester evaluations reflect this context honestly. They are not the scores of IIM Ranchi, and they should not be read as if they were. What they do show is consistent punctuality, genuine enthusiasm for the subject matter, and a majority of students who would recommend the courses to others — even in a semester defined by institutional uncertainty and out-of-domain assignments.

The more meaningful question is what happens when I teach within my domain, with a prepared cohort, in a stable institutional environment. That answer is already in this portfolio. The SAU data is the starting point of a new arc, not a revision of the one that preceded it.

The Growth Arc: Five Phases

1
2018 to 2022 · Geneva and HSE Moscow
Foundation
  • Teaching assistant at Geneva GSEM. Scores on a 1-4 scale; average 3.58/4.00 = 89.5% of maximum.
  • Availability rated at or near 4.00/4.00 in every evaluation. The defining strength from day one.
  • DDDM scores improved from 3.43 to 3.61 between 2018 and 2020, the earliest visible feedback-to-growth loop.
  • HSE Moscow: R and Python taught to Russian undergraduates entirely online. Novelty scores 4.22/5, confirming content relevance.
Geneva note: Branding Spring 2018 scored 3.88/4.00 = 97% of maximum. The 1-4 scale context matters when reading these numbers.
2
2022 · IIM Bodhgaya, first year
Early peaks and first friction
  • Principles of Marketing Management (IPM, Jan 2022): course 4.74, preparation 4.87. Immediate traction with undergraduate cohorts.
  • Rural Marketing (PGP, Mar 2022): instructor 4.76. Life-skill framing lands hardest in contextual, case-rich courses.
  • PGP MBA courses: a proxy attendance incident in a large cohort creates a mid-semester climate shift that the data captures clearly.
Key finding: undergraduate students respond more strongly to inspiration-led pedagogy. PGP MBA students carry stronger prior expectations and need a different classroom contract. This distinction shapes everything that follows.
3
2023 · IIM Bodhgaya, consolidation and crisis
The most diagnostic year
  • Record highs in repeat courses: POMM IPM reaches 4.97 preparation, Rural Marketing stays strong.
  • Business Research Methods (IPM, Mar 2023): lowest scores in the dataset. Instructor 2.94, respect score 2.50. A single classroom incident created lasting anxiety for the rest of the semester.
  • Recovery visible within the same year: Brand Management 4.13, Foundation Marketing MBA 4.68.
Critical pattern: preparation never drops below 3.95 even at the dataset's lowest point. The crisis was a classroom relationship issue, not a knowledge deficit. That distinction matters when reading this data honestly.
4
2025 · IIM Ranchi
Resolution and peak
  • Preparation 5.00/5.00, unanimous across all 29 respondents. Zero variance. A first in the entire dataset.
  • Instructor vs. peers: 4.86 vs. 4.72. Exam feedback: 25/29 enjoyed it, 0 said useless.
  • Students from engineering and finance backgrounds report permanent shifts in how they see marketing.
Confirmed formula: maximum intellectual rigour plus explicitly protected psychological safety plus consistent interactive tools equals the highest scores in eight years. This is the floor going forward, not the ceiling.
5
2025 to present · South Asian University · Greenfield
New institution, new variables
  • First semester at a brand-new SAARC university. Taught Indian Ethos and Business Ethics (MBA, 24 respondents) and IT and Systems Lab (BBA, 48 respondents) — both outside primary domain, assigned by institutional need.
  • IEBS: 71% rated course coverage Excellent or Good; 63% would recommend; teacher attitude rated Enthusiastic or Positive by 63%. Oral presentation rated Excellent by 54%.
  • IT Lab: 55% would recommend; 56% rated teacher attitude Positive or Enthusiastic; availability rated Good or Excellent by 52%.
  • Consistent punctuality and regularity noted across both courses by students.
Reading this data correctly: these scores reflect a first semester in a greenfield institution, teaching out-of-domain subjects, with a first-year cohort admitted under conditions not controlled by the faculty. The positive signals — recommendation rates, enthusiasm ratings, punctuality — are the ones that travel across contexts. The gaps are specific to this configuration and are already being addressed as the institution matures and domain-aligned teaching resumes.

What eight years of data shows
  • Preparation is the one score that never moved. Across 16 courses, 6 countries, marketing and analytics, undergraduate and MBA, in-person and online: preparation is consistently the highest-rated dimension. Knowledge and effort are dispositional, not contextual.
  • When psychological safety and preparation coexist, scores are exceptional. The IIM Ranchi data is not an outlier; it is proof of what happens when the full formula is applied consistently. 5.00 preparation is not luck. It is unanimous across 29 independent respondents.
  • Context shapes scores more than competence does. The SAU first semester — greenfield institution, out-of-domain assignments, first-year cohort — produced scores that reflect those conditions. The same educator produced 5.00/5.00 preparation at IIM Ranchi three months earlier. Both data points are true. Reading them together is more informative than reading either alone.
